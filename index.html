DECADES OF INK

The Statesman presses, going quiet as printing operations
shift elsewhere, forever hum to the men and women who
worked on them night after night.

Story by Nancy Flores
Title photo by Reshma Kirpalani
Presentation by Paighten Harkins
Video by Kelly West, Reshma Kirpalani

Moments after Independence Day fireworks exploded over Lady Bird Lake, revelers poured down South Congress Avenue past the American-Statesman building, many eager to continue their Saturday night festivities.

The Austin American-Statesman pressroom has four presses, which will be dismantled as all print operations at the newspaper end. (Rodolfo Gonzalez/American-Statesman)

But inside the cavernous pressroom on Fourth of July, it was crunch time.

Pressroom operators raced against the clock to print more than 110,000 copies of Sunday’s newspaper, the biggest of the week. SUV-sized rolls of newsprint fed into the hulking metal machines that would print the day’s news, fold and eventually whisk away finished copies on a conveyor system hanging from the ceiling. As the clocked ticked, the rhythmic vibrations pulsated throughout the sprawling space.

When the night’s first test copies began to print, press workers furiously scanned the newspaper pages. Their trained eyes searched out the smallest imperfections, such as overly darkened columns or pages slightly askew. They hustled to make adjustments from a computerized control room.

Scan, scan, adjust. Each copy printed better than the last.

At 10:48 p.m., while holiday traffic snarled outside, the first good copy of Sunday’s front page rolled off the press.

Mark Morrell manually feeds newsprint through a press for an afternoon run on July 15. Morrell has worked in the pressrooms for the past 20 years. He is not sure what he will do after leaving. (Reshma Kirpalani/American-Statesman)

“Final edition,” pressroom supervisor Joe Mireles said into his walkie talkie. “Let’s make it look good.”

It was the last American-Statesman front page printed in the newspaper’s Austin printing facility, and for these workers and the city, the night marked the end of an era.

For nearly 150 years, a version of the newspaper, which was founded in 1871 as the Democratic Statesman and changed names several times until it became the Austin American-Statesman in 1973, has been printed locally.

In April, the Statesman announced it would no longer print its own newspapers but outsource its printing operations to San Antonio and Houston as a cost-saving measure. Even with the change, the paper will continue seven-day-a-week print publication and delivery.

The shift will result in the layoffs of about 100 pressroom and packaging workers. The Statesman press and packaging workers, many of whom have been in the printing industry for generations, have developed family-like bonds over the decades as they have kept a unique art form alive.

NIGHT OWLS

Pressroom planner Chuck Patterson removes from the press an aluminum printing plate of the last locally printed front page of the American-Statesman just after midnight on July 4. The press run had just concluded, and Patterson was claiming the plate of the front page as a memento. (Zach Ryall/American-Statesman)

When pressroom planner Chuck Patterson first walked into the Statesman pressroom in December 1979, he took a look around at the massive machinery and said, “Oh my gosh. It takes all this to make a newspaper?”

At 19, he’d made it his goal to work at the Statesman pressroom. He was hired in 1980.

It takes a certain personality to do this, Patterson says, especially when you’re told, “OK, you’re going to work nights, weekends and holidays. And you’re going to get real dirty.”

Over the next 35 years, he saw many people come and go. Press operators start their careers spending about four to six years working as apprentices, mastering every aspect of the job, from learning how to feed the newsprint through the presses to making color registration adjustments on each issue.

Rolls of newsprint, some weighing more than 1,000 pounds, roll around on a mechanical train system underneath the press room, where they are fed into the printing presses. (Kelly West/ American-Statesman)

Although crews work around the clock on the various publications that have used the Statesman presses, it’s at night when the newspaper’s front-page stories print.
Sometimes, after working through the night, the workers would pick up some beer to unwind. But buying booze at 7 a.m. sometimes meant getting the stink eye at the store from cashiers and customers. It became routine to explain to folks that 7 a.m. in their world was more like 7 p.m.

For press operator Jason Perkins, one benefit of the grueling schedule was being able to spend mornings with his children. “When they got up to go to school, I was there,” says Perkins, who joined the Statesman in 1985. “I could take them to their doctor’s appointments and could go on field trips during the day when other people couldn’t.”

Rick Hill, right, checks a copy of the Round Rock Leader while Frank Mata cleans blankets and strip plates off of press cylinders on July 15. Hill said he will continue his side career in the air conditioning business after he leaves the Statesman. Mata plans to go back to school to learn a new trade. (Reshma Kirpalani/American-Statesman)

LIKE A FAMILY

The crew that printed the final Austin-run edition of the American-Statesman poses for a photo at the end of the night on July 4. More than 100 press and packaging workers will lose their jobs when all print operations finally end at the paper. (Deborah Cannon/American-Statesman)

Over the years, deep bonds began to form between the workers. In the wee hours of the morning, they’d drive to the coast to go fishing or head to Six Flags as those working regular business hours were stuck in traffic.

“It’s like a brotherhood,” Patterson says. Sometimes you had to have thick skin and stand behind buddies going through tough times.

There’s typically not a lot of turnover, says pressroom manager Ronnie Hampton, who has been in the printing industry for 44 years. “So it’s pretty much a family.”

That was the case for press operators Liz and Wallace Furber, who met in the pressroom and have been married for 31 years. When their children visited, they thought the giant spools of newsprint were huge toilet paper rolls.

One of the toughest parts about the outsourcing, Hampton says, is watching the pressroom family deteriorate.

The family also extends to packaging workers, who handle preprinted inserts and ready the paper for distribution. “It’s bittersweet in that you like to see technology and you like to see things change,” says Statesman packaging manager Eloy Gonzales. “But also knowing that that is what’s causing you to shut down your operation … it’s hard. We spend a lot of time together.”

Inky fingerprints cover the walls in the pressroom at the Austin American-Statesman. "You will literally head-to-toe be covered in ink," press operator Kyle Tullos says. "There's no time to be clean." (Kelly West/American-Statesman)

INK IN YOUR BLOOD

Press foreman Keith Rutherford works on a printing unit in the American-Statesman pressroom on June 22. Press workers have to learn the intricacies of each machine they work with in order to keep them running as efficiently as possible. (Reshma Kirpalani/American-Statesman)

Over the years, longtime press workers have witnessed the shifts in the printing industry, including transitions from letterpress printing, the oldest of the traditional printing techniques, to offset printing, which transfers an image from an etched aluminum plate to a blanket roller and finally to newsprint.

As printing operations wind down at the Statesman, two out of the four presses, now computerized and software-driven, will continue to roll under existing contracts with other publications including a regional edition of the New York Times and the Waco Tribune-Herald until 2017. Plans for the other two presses haven’t been finalized.

Some press operators came to the Statesman with family printing ties and a familiarity with the machines. Others learned on the job.

Pressroom supervisor Mireles’ first summer job was working in his uncle’s print shop in San Antonio, where he printed things like business cards and notepads. “It’s in my blood,” Mireles says. “I’m so used to the ink, the smell — and what you’re smelling (in a pressroom) is the paper, oil, ink, paper dust all in one place. When my wife does the laundry, she can always smell it.”

Before 1985, Perkins had never been in a pressroom. At 19, he’d been working at Furr’s Cafeteria, and the promise of earning $6 an hour back then was enough to pique his interest in printing.

Old canisters of spot color are stored in the back of the pressroom at the American-Statesman. The press uses a four color printing process of cyan, magenta, yellow and black inks, which can be mixed to create other colors. (Kelly West/American-Statesman)

Over the decades, he became part of the pressroom family and mastered the craft.

When Patterson, who was working at a K-Mart in San Angelo, moved to Austin at 19 “it was like going to another world,” he says. “It was so fast-paced here. I was like a kid in a candy store.”

Patterson grew up out in the country and says most of the people working in the pressroom at night had come off the farm from places like Hutto and Taylor.

“We just grew up together,” he says. “I learned a lot about life working here. You’ll never learn that stuff in school, college or anywhere else. I was a kid when I got here, and the guys enlightened me to what life was about.”

WITNESSING HISTORY

Newsprint flies through the KBA press in the American-Statesman's press room. At their peak, the Statesman presses can print about 60,000 copies an hour. (Reshma Kirpalani/American-Statesman)

On the night of Fourth of July, press operators climbed onto the massive machines to fix paper tears or anything else that prevented the presses from flying. At their peak, the Statesman presses can print about 60,000 copies an hour.

Workers are accustomed to moving quickly — it’s the news business after all. And, over the decades, they’ve been running behind the scenes during the historical moments that have touched us all.

Patterson keeps a stack of significant newspapers in his closet — the editions when Stevie Ray Vaughan died, when Space Shuttle Challenger exploded and Sept. 11, 2001. But printing the newspaper on election night 2000 during the contentious presidential race between Vice President Al Gore and then-Texas Gov. George W. Bush was “like being at the movies,” he says.

The Nov. 8, 2000 edition of the Austin American-Statesman, printed after the 2000 presidential election between Vice President Al Gore and then-Texas Gov. George W. Bush. Pressmen had to stop the presses twice that election night.

Twice that night he was told by a Statesman senior editor to stop the presses, and Patterson remembers printing four front pages that ranged from declaring Bush the winner to the final headline: “History on Hold.”

It was the first time since he’d worked at the Statesman that newspapers were called back to the building.

“They had papers at the Capitol, and they were hawking them in the streets,” he says. “We couldn’t get those back.”

A copy of the last main edition of the Statesman that was printed locally rests on a stack of rags at the end of the night on Saturday, July 4.“You know how I look at it?” says Mark Wilson, the Statesman’s director of fulfillment. “We’re like a construction company. We build a brand new home from a blueprint. ... When we’re done, we tear it down and build another new home the next day. And we do that every single day." (Kelly West/American-Statesman)

END OF AN ERA

Lalo Gonzalez throws away a test copy of the Statesman during the final main run of the paper at the American-Statesman on July 4. Checking the printed products for imperfections is part of the press workers' routine with every new run. (Kelly West/American-Statesman)

When Patterson goes to the grocery store, he can’t help but notice the color registration on the packaging of items like potato chip bags. “I’ve gotten paper towels before and thought ‘What is this? The colors aren’t lined up correctly,’ ” he says. “Most common people don’t come close to seeing what a pressman sees on something that’s been printed.”

Although he’s worked his way up from being a press operator, Patterson says no matter what job title he’s earned, he’ll always identify as a pressman.

For Chris Wolfe, 43, being able to print the newspaper’s front page over the years has been a career highlight. He still remembers watching “Batman” episodes where spinning newspapers appeared on the screen and knowing that’s what he wanted to do. Although he’ll miss it, he’s decided to move to Florida and dedicate himself to a new career after leaving the Statesman pressroom.

“You know how I look at it?” says Mark Wilson, the Statesman’s director of fulfillment. “We’re like a construction company. We build a brand new home from a blueprint… . When we’re done, we tear it down and build another new home the next day. And we do that every single day. I don’t know how many people out there do that. Who builds a home every day?”

At 12:15 a.m. on July 5, the roaring presses fell quiet as they came to a halt. Mireles signaled to his ink-covered crew the end of another successful run. “Good job, fellas,” he said. “We’re done.”
